2. Data Collection & Insights

There are many different types of point of sale terminals. They include:


    - The simple stationary register, with a cash drawer, credit card reader, and barcode scanner

    - Open-source POS machines with product icons that can be tapped

    - Cloud-based mobile POS machines which use a digital application to conduct business

    - Self-service kiosks (usually tablets) which allow consumers to select their payment and receipt delivery method, as well as make additional purchases

    Mobile POS is a portable computer device that is used especially as POS (Point of Sales) purpose and it is link to input and output device such as barcode scanner and thermal printer, installed with special software for POS. This paper is a proposed two types design of implementation Mobile Point of Sale (MPOS), which are using Mini PC Raspberry Pi and Smartphone Android OS. Both solution passed the functional test and the comparison between both is performed, which is shows that Raspberry Pi has more economical solution but more difficult to be developed, less comfort and less friendly use than Android Smart Phone based implementation.


mPOS (mobile point-of-sale):


MPOS, or mobile point-of-sale, is a smartphone, tablet or dedicated wireless device that performs the functions of a cash register or electronic POS terminal wirelessly. MPOS solutions require little setup compared to traditional POS. Traditional POS systems will usually use a mini desktop computer connected to a barcode scanner, touchscreen monitor, cash drawer and receipt printer. MPOS can consist of as little as a normal smartphone with a credit card reader and a 5G cell service. This lets mPOS have much less setup cost and they can be used anywhere.


How does an mPOS work?:


Just about any smartphone or tablet can be converted to an mPOS terminal. The business will sign up with the mPOS provider and download an app onto the device. Typically, a hardware card reader will be plugged into the device's USB-C or lightning port to process near-field communication (NFC) or chip payments. Some readers also provide barcode scanners and receipt printers. Older card readers that use the device's audio jack have been largely phased out. Some can be optionally connected to a Bluetooth receipt printer.


Modern mPOS apps can utilie the smartphone's built-in NFC reader without any additional hardware readers. This can be called tap-on-phone or software POS.


Often, the mPOS provider also acts as the credit card payment processor. No customer or payment data is stored directly on the device. The payments can then be directly deposited into the business' bank account. Other systems can be integrated into a traditional POS system. Some systems also offer entire business management systems with sales, inventory tracking, customer loyalty, returns, gift cards and individual employee accounts.

Key Findings & Considerations

1. Business needs & industry-specific features

  • Retail: Inventory management, barcode scanning, and multi-location support.
  • Restaurant: Table management, online ordering, and kitchen display integration.
  • Service-Based: Appointment scheduling, customer databases, and invoicing.

2. Ease of use

  • User-friendly interface for quick adoption.
  • Fast transaction processing to minimise wait times.
  • Easy onboarding for employees.

3. Hardware compatibility

  • Supports necessary peripherals (card readers, barcode scanners, receipt printers).
  • Cloud-based vs. on-premise hardware requirements.
  • Compatibility with existing devices (iPads, Android tablets, etc.).

4. Payment processing options

  • Accepts multiple payment methods (credit/debit cards, digital wallets, BNPL).
  • Integration with preferred payment gateways.
  • Transparent pricing for transaction fees.

5. Software features & integrations

  • Inventory tracking and automatic stock updates.
  • CRM (Customer Relationship Management) for loyalty programs.
  • Integration with accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero, etc.).
  • Reporting and analytics for sales insights.

6. Scalability

  • Can grow with the business (multi-location support, additional users).
  • Cloud-based solutions for remote management.

7. Security & compliance

  • PCI compliance for secure payments.
  • Data encryption to protect customer information.
  • Fraud prevention features.

8. Cost & pricing model

  • Monthly subscription vs. one-time purchase.
  • Transaction fees and additional charges.
  • Cost of hardware and upgrades.

9. Customer support & reliability

  • 24/7 support availability.
  • Online resources and training materials.
  • System uptime and reliability.

What have I learned

Problem framing is half the solution: Clearly defining the core problem upfront made every design decision more purposeful.

Stakeholder interviews provide hidden gems Talking to internal teams revealed edge cases and overlooked workflows.

Quantitative + qualitative = powerful: Pairing analytics with user feedback gave a fuller picture of user behaviour.

Low-fidelity wins early: Sketching and wireframing saved time and kept the team focused on functionality over aesthetics.

Moodboards align vision: Visual inspiration boards helped align the team’s creative direction early in the process.

Progressive disclosure matters: Giving users information gradually improved comprehension and engagement.

Consistency builds trust: Reusing UI patterns made the product more intuitive and polished.
